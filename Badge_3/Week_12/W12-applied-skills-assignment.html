⚖️ Component 4: Legal and Business Case

Source lessons: L20 (Legal Frameworks), L21 (Business Case Development) | Outcomes: 3E.01-04, 3F.01-03

Build the case for addressing the problem you identified in Component 1. This has two parts: legal exposure and business rationale. Both should be specific to your product, not generic statements about accessibility being important.

Part A: Legal Exposure

  • Applicable standards: Which legal frameworks apply to your product? (Section 508, EN 301 549, European Accessibility Act, others?) Why?
  • Exposure mapping: Connect your Component 1 findings to specific legal requirements. Which of your identified issues create compliance risk?
  • Risk framing: Distinguish between legal obligation and best practice. Not everything is a legal risk — be precise about what is.

Part B: Business Rationale

  • Audience: Who makes resource decisions for your product? Frame the case for that person.
  • Business impact: Connect accessibility issues to business outcomes — user retention, market access, support costs, competitive positioning, or whatever applies to your context.
  • Cost of inaction: What happens if these issues are not addressed? Be specific.

Rubric guidance:

A 2 correctly identifies applicable frameworks and states general business value. A 3 maps specific findings to specific legal requirements and frames the business case for the actual decision-maker. A 4 does all of that, distinguishes legal obligation from best practice, quantifies business impact where possible, and presents a case a stakeholder could act on immediately.

🗺️ Component 5: Product Strategy Recommendation

Source lesson: L22 (Product Strategy Advocacy) | Outcomes: 3G.01, 3G.02, 3G.03

Draft a concrete recommendation for your product. This is not "we should be more accessible." It is a specific, actionable proposal connected to your product's development process.

Choose one (or combine):

  • Acceptance criteria: Write accessibility acceptance criteria for a feature or component your team is building or maintaining.
  • Roadmap input: Draft a proposal for adding accessibility work to your product's roadmap. Include scope, priority rationale, and suggested timeline.
  • Early-intervention proposal: Identify a point in your team's design process where accessibility input would prevent the most downstream issues, and propose how to integrate it.

What makes it actionable:

  • It references your actual product, not a hypothetical
  • It connects to findings from Component 1 and the case from Component 4
  • Someone on your team could read it and know what to do
  • It accounts for how your team actually works (sprint cycles, review processes, tooling)

Rubric guidance:

A 2 provides a recommendation that is relevant but vague or disconnected from the team's actual workflow. A 3 provides a specific, actionable recommendation grounded in the product context. A 4 provides a recommendation that has already been shared with the team or stakeholders and includes their feedback or response.
