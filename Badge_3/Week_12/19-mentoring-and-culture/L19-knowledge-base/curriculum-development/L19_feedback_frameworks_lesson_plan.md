# L19: Mentoring and Culture - Lesson Plan

**Course:** DACE
**Badge:** Deliver
**Delivery:** In-person (instructor-led)
**Duration:** ~20 minutes (Week 11 session: 30 min review + 60 min across L17, L18, L19)
**Outcomes:** 3H.01, 3H.02, 3H.03

---

## Lesson Overview

This lesson moves from giving good accessibility feedback to building accessibility capability at scale. Learners are expected to support other designers, create repeatable learning structures, and improve the standards and processes that shape product work.

**Why This Matters:** One strong accessibility reviewer is useful. A team that can reliably design with accessibility in mind is far more valuable. Badge 3 leadership means creating durable capability, not just solving individual problems.

**Connection to L18:** L18 focused on high-quality critique. L19 extends that work into mentoring, community-building, and organizational standards.

## Content Outline

### Opening: Capability Beyond Yourself (2 min)

**Key Teaching Points:**
- Accessibility progress stalls when it depends on one person.
- Badge 3 leadership means making other people stronger, not just becoming the expert in the room.
- Mentoring, communities of practice, and standards all extend accessibility capability beyond individual reviews.

---

### Section 1: Mentoring by Skill Level (7 min)

**Outcomes:** 3H.01

**Key Teaching Points:**

1. **Different starting points require different support**
   - Discover Badge designers need fundamentals and pattern recognition
   - Define Badge designers need help connecting design choices to AT behavior and validation
   - Untrained designers need simple, concrete entry points

2. **Mentoring moment vs review moment**
   - Mentoring builds understanding
   - Review evaluates the work and establishes action
   - Confusing the two leads to weak critique or weak coaching

3. **Leadership qualities**
   - Patience: explain without rushing people past the learning step
   - Empathy: understand where the confusion or resistance is coming from
   - Persistence: keep the standard high even when progress is slow

**Discussion prompt:**
"How would you explain the same accessibility issue differently to a Discover Badge designer and a senior designer with no DACE training?"

---

### Section 2: Communities of Practice (6 min)

**Outcomes:** 3H.02

**Key Teaching Points:**

1. **Why communities matter**
   - They keep accessibility visible between formal reviews
   - They make knowledge shareable instead of person-dependent
   - They help teams normalize accessibility discussions early

2. **What a community of practice can look like**
   - recurring critique sessions
   - shared examples and patterns
   - onboarding pathways
   - lightweight teaching moments inside existing design rituals

3. **Culture change**
   - Culture changes when standards, language, and expectations become routine
   - Accessibility becomes durable when teams see it as product quality, not an add-on

---

### Section 3: Standards and Organizational Contribution (4 min)

**Outcomes:** 3H.03

**Key Teaching Points:**

1. **Standards and process are leverage**
   - Documentation, templates, and process guidance reduce repeated explanation
   - Better standards raise the baseline before critique even begins

2. **Contribution opportunities**
   - improve onboarding materials
   - document recurring findings
   - capture reusable examples
   - clarify acceptance criteria and pattern guidance

3. **DEI connection**
   - Accessibility is part of building inclusive systems, not just compliant features

---

### Close (1 min)

- "Accessibility leadership is not just expertise. It is the ability to make accessibility more likely in the work of other people."
- Transition to Week 12: "Next we move into the organizational case for accessibility, including legal frameworks and business rationale."

## Homework Connection

Ask learners to identify one practical contribution they could make in the next two weeks:

- mentor a less-experienced designer on a real issue
- start or join a recurring accessibility check-in
- improve one piece of onboarding or documentation

They should bring that idea into the capstone or PEAS observation reflection.

## Instructor Notes

### Pacing

Keep the lesson concrete. Avoid turning it into generic leadership advice. Every point should connect back to accessibility practice, review quality, onboarding, or organizational standards.

If running short:
- Compress the communities-of-practice section to one example and one takeaway

If running long:
- Add one quick scenario comparing a mentoring response and a review response

### Common Questions

**Q: Isn't mentoring just part of feedback?**  
A: Feedback can be transactional and tied to a specific artifact. Mentoring is broader. It develops long-term judgment and capability.

**Q: What if I am not formally responsible for standards?**  
A: You can still improve examples, templates, onboarding notes, and recurring review language. Standards are often improved incrementally.

**Q: How do communities of practice start without a formal program?**  
A: Start small: a recurring review, a shared channel, a critique rotation, or a reusable examples library.
