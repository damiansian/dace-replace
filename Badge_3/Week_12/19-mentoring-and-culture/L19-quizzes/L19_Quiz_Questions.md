## Question 1

**Outcome:** 3H.01
**Type:** Application

**Stem:**
> A Discover Badge designer asks for help understanding why visible labels must match accessible names. What is the best mentoring approach?

- A. Give only the WCAG number and ask them to research it
- B. Explain the principle in concrete terms, connect it to user impact, and use a simple example they can apply
- C. Assume they already know the concept and move directly to advanced AT testing details
- D. Tell them to wait until they complete Badge 3 before asking questions

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Mentoring should be calibrated to the learner's current level. A Discover Badge designer needs clear fundamentals, concrete examples, and a direct connection to user impact.

---

## Question 2

**Outcome:** 3H.01
**Type:** Scenario

**Stem:**
> During a design review, you stop to explain a core accessibility concept in depth even though the team still needs a clear decision on the current design. Which distinction are you failing to maintain?

- A. Technical accuracy vs legal risk
- B. Mentoring moment vs review moment
- C. Business case vs user impact
- D. Pattern selection vs implementation detail

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Mentoring moments build understanding. Review moments evaluate the work and define action. Both matter, but confusing them can derail a review or leave the team without a decision.

## Question 3

**Outcome:** 3H.02
**Type:** Recall

**Stem:**
> What is the main value of an accessibility community of practice?

- A. It centralizes all accessibility decisions in one team so product teams do less work
- B. It sustains shared knowledge and attention so accessibility does not depend on one champion
- C. It replaces critique sessions with asynchronous documentation
- D. It removes the need for onboarding and mentoring

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Communities of practice help keep accessibility visible, shared, and repeatable across teams. Their purpose is to sustain capability beyond any one individual.

## Question 4

**Outcome:** 3H.03
**Type:** Application

**Stem:**
> Which contribution best fits the outcome "Contribute to organizational standards"?

- A. Quietly fixing one design file without documenting the pattern
- B. Creating a reusable checklist or guidance note that helps multiple teams avoid the same accessibility issue
- C. Waiting for a formal leadership role before improving any process
- D. Focusing only on your own product area and ignoring shared documentation

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Contributing to organizational standards means improving documentation, guidance, templates, or process in ways that raise the accessibility baseline beyond one immediate artifact.

---

## Question 5

**Outcome:** 3H.02
**Type:** Scenario

**Stem:**
> A team says accessibility progress keeps stalling whenever the most experienced reviewer is unavailable. What is the best Badge 3 response?

- A. Ask that reviewer to attend every critique from now on
- B. Build stronger mentoring, onboarding, and shared accessibility practices so knowledge is distributed
- C. Reduce accessibility expectations so more people can keep up
- D. Stop doing critiques until the expert has more time

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Badge 3 leadership is about building durable capability. If progress depends on one person, the right move is to improve mentoring, communities of practice, onboarding, and standards so the knowledge becomes shared.
