⚖️ Component 4: Legal and Business Case

Source lessons: L20 (Legal Frameworks), L21 (Business Case) | Outcomes: 3E.01-04, 3F.01-03

Build the case for addressing the problem you identified in Component 1. This is how you move from "we have issues" to "here is why we should invest." You need two halves: the legal exposure and the business rationale.

What to include:

  • Legal exposure: Map the legal frameworks relevant to your product segment (ADA, Section 508, AODA, EAA, or others depending on your product's markets). Identify which of your Component 1 findings create compliance risk and at what level.
  • Business rationale: Frame the case for the audience who makes resource decisions. Connect accessibility investment to business outcomes your stakeholders care about — user retention, market access, support cost reduction, brand risk, or competitive positioning.
  • Cost of inaction: What happens if these issues remain unaddressed? Be specific — not "we might get sued" but which specific findings create which specific risks.
  • Recommended investment level: Are you asking for a focused fix, a systematic program, or a strategic shift? Scale your ask to what the evidence supports.

Rubric guidance:

A 2 lists legal requirements and general business benefits without connecting them to the product's specific situation. A 3 maps legal exposure to specific findings and builds a business case using data relevant to the product's stakeholders. A 4 does all of that and quantifies risk or opportunity in terms the decision-maker would use (cost, timeline, competitive impact).

🎯 Component 5: Product Strategy Recommendation

Source lesson: L22 (Product Strategy Integration) | Outcomes: 3G.01, 3G.02, 3G.03

Draft a concrete recommendation for your product. This is not "we should be more accessible." It is a specific, actionable recommendation connected to your product's development process.

Pick one of these formats (or propose your own):

  • Acceptance criteria update: Draft accessibility acceptance criteria for a specific feature or component. Show what "done" looks like when accessibility is included.
  • Roadmap input: Propose a prioritized set of accessibility improvements for the next 1-2 quarters, tied to the severity findings from Component 1.
  • Early-intervention proposal: Describe a process change that catches accessibility issues earlier in your team's design-to-development pipeline.
  • Integration recommendation: Propose how accessibility checkpoints should fit into your product's existing review, QA, or release process.

What makes it count:

  • Specific: Names the feature, component, or process. Not "the product" — the actual thing.
  • Actionable: Someone could read this and know what to do next.
  • Connected: Ties back to Component 1 findings and Component 4 rationale.
  • Realistic: Sized to what your team could actually adopt. A perfect plan nobody uses is worth less than a good plan they adopt.

Rubric guidance:

A 2 makes a general recommendation without connecting it to specific findings or the product's actual development process. A 3 drafts a specific, actionable recommendation tied to Component 1 data and the team's real workflow. A 4 does all of that and demonstrates understanding of how the recommendation fits into the broader product roadmap and organizational dynamics.
