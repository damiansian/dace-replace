### Frame 1.2: Four parts of a strong business case

**Type:** Four-grid

**Headline:** Core parts of the accessibility business case

**Cards:**
- **User reach:** Who benefits and how many users are affected
- **Legal risk:** What exposure or compliance pressure exists
- **Brand value:** Trust, reputation, and product credibility
- **Efficiency gains:** Reduced rework, lower support burden, better system quality

**Key insight callout:**
If the case only says "this matters," it is incomplete. It needs a business consequence.

**Speaker notes:**
These four dimensions give learners a repeatable structure. Not every case uses all four equally, but most strong accessibility arguments use at least two or three.

---

### Frame 1.3: Cost of inaction

**Type:** Two-column

**Headline:** Accessibility debt has a real price

**Left column:**
- Heading: `If we wait`
- Rework gets more expensive
- Legal exposure grows
- Customer frustration compounds
- Support and exception handling increase

**Right column:**
- Heading: `If we invest now`
- Remediation is cheaper
- Quality improves earlier
- Teams build better defaults
- Trust and retention strengthen

**Speaker notes:**
The cost-of-inaction argument matters because some teams only react to visible downside. This is still not fear-based messaging. It is consequence-based messaging.

### Frame 2.2: What to measure

**Type:** Three-column

**Headline:** Useful ROI signals

**Content:**
- Trend metrics: issue volume, severity, time to remediation
- Product metrics: task success, support burden, retention signals
- Process metrics: fewer regressions, less rework, stronger definition of done

**Key insight callout:**
ROI communication gets stronger when it shows direction over time, not a single isolated number.

**Speaker notes:**
Learners do not need perfect attribution models. They need defensible indicators that show progress, risk reduction, or operational efficiency.

---

### Frame 2.3: From metrics to leadership language

**Type:** Two-column

**Headline:** Translate technical progress into business language

**Left column:**
- Heading: `What not to say`
- "We fixed 14 accessibility bugs"
- "Screen reader output is cleaner now"
- "We improved semantic markup"

**Right column:**
- Heading: `What leaders hear`
- Reduced critical barriers in a core flow
- Lower risk in a regulated market
- Fewer expensive late-stage fixes

**Speaker notes:**
The work can stay technically accurate, but the framing should match the audience. Technical evidence stays underneath the business statement.

### Frame 3.2: Choosing the strongest components

**Type:** Two-column

**Headline:** Not every case leads with the same lens

**Left column:**
- Heading: `High legal exposure`
- Lead with compliance risk and cost of remediation
- Support with user reach and brand

**Right column:**
- Heading: `Low legal exposure`
- Lead with user reach and product quality
- Support with efficiency gains and rework reduction

**Key insight callout:**
Match the lead argument to the situation. The four lenses stay available — the emphasis shifts.

**Speaker notes:**
This helps learners move beyond a one-size-fits-all business case. The components from Section 1 are tools. This frame teaches when to reach for which one.

---

### Frame 3.3: Putting it together

**Type:** Summary

**Headline:** A complete business case combines three layers

**Bullet list:**
- Business-case lenses: user reach, legal risk, brand value, efficiency
- ROI signals: trend metrics, product metrics, process metrics
- Cost of inaction: what gets more expensive if the work is deferred

**Key insight callout:**
If any layer is missing, the case is easier to defer. All three together make prioritization more likely.

**Speaker notes:**
This gives learners a mental template for the artifact they would actually produce. L22 will focus on deploying this case in live planning conversations.
