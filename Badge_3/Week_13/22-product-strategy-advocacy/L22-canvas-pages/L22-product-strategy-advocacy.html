Lesson 22: Product Strategy Advocacy

🚀 Deliver Badge 🗺️ Module 3G

The strongest accessibility work happens before detailed design and implementation lock in costly decisions. This lesson focuses on shaping roadmap conversations, writing usable acceptance criteria, and intervening early enough to prevent accessibility debt.

Badge 3 learners need to influence what gets built, not only review what already exists. Product strategy advocacy is where accessibility becomes preventive rather than reactive.

Key Concepts

Roadmap influence 3G.01

Accessibility needs to appear in planning conversations while scope, timing, and dependencies are still adjustable. If it enters only during review, the team is already paying a higher remediation cost.

Useful planning signals include known barrier patterns, likely debt in upcoming features, dependency risk, and compliance or market obligations that affect release decisions.

Different roles in the room respond to different emphasis. Executives hear risk, market reach, and brand. PMs hear roadmap impact, prioritization, and user value. Engineers hear implementation timing, debt reduction, and rework avoidance. The facts stay stable — the emphasis changes based on what each audience acts on.

✓ Checkpoint

  • I can explain why planning-stage influence matters
  • I can name signals to raise in roadmap discussions
  • I can adapt the accessibility message for executives, PMs, or engineers

Acceptance criteria 3G.02

"Must be accessible" is not a usable acceptance criterion. Strong criteria describe expected behavior in a way that can be validated in design review, implementation, or QA.

Good criteria connect accessibility to definition of done. They turn values into accountability.

✓ Checkpoint

  • I can distinguish vague language from testable criteria
  • I can write one feature-level accessibility acceptance criterion

Early intervention 3G.03

The same accessibility problem is cheaper and easier to address at concept stage than after design and implementation are already committed. Badge 3 learners need to recognize when a direction is creating debt before that debt becomes normalized.

When pushback happens, make the tradeoff explicit, re-anchor on user impact, and offer an alternative path where possible.

✓ Checkpoint

  • I can identify when a feature needs early accessibility intervention
  • I can make accessibility debt visible in a planning discussion

Homework

Choose one upcoming feature in your product area and draft: one planning-stage accessibility risk, one testable acceptance criterion, and one point where early intervention would reduce later debt.
