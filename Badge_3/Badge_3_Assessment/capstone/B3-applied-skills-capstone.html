Applied Skills Capstone

Your product. Your data. Your case for change.

🚀 Deliver Badge 📋 Weeks 11-15 🏆 Required for Badge 3

The Applied Skills Capstone is a portfolio built from your real product work across the Deliver Badge unit. It is not a final paper written in Week 14. It is assembled piece by piece, starting this week, from assignments that each produce a component of the finished portfolio.

By the end of Week 15, you will have a complete body of evidence showing you can identify accessibility problems in your product, communicate findings effectively, build the business case, and contribute to lasting change. This is the sole requirement for earning the Deliver Badge and the title of Accessibility Champion.

Every weekly assignment is a capstone component. If you do the work each week, the capstone assembles itself.

The Six Components

Each component is produced through a weekly assignment. Links to each assignment page will be added as the weeks progress.

Component 1: Problem Definition

Week 11

Using Jira data, the accessibility dashboard, or both, identify and document the accessibility problem your product faces. Classify findings by severity. Ground everything in data, not assumptions.

Learning outcomes: 3A.01, 3A.02, 3A.03 | Source lesson: L17

Component 2: PEAS Session Reflection

Weeks 11-14

Attend, observe, or watch a recorded PEAS session. Write a structured reflection that documents what happened, connects at least one finding to your own product using SBI or COIN, and recommends a next step for your team.

Learning outcomes: 3A.01, 3A.02, 3A.03, 3B.01, 3B.02, 3B.03 | Source lessons: L17, L18

Component 3: Contribution Artifact

Week 11

Create one reusable accessibility resource for your team: a checklist, pattern guide, onboarding material, shared example, or process improvement. It must be specific to your team's patterns and practical enough to use during real work.

Learning outcomes: 3H.01, 3H.02, 3H.03 | Source lesson: L19

Component 4: Legal and Business Case

Week 12

Build the case for addressing the problem you identified in Component 1. Map the legal exposure for your product segment. Frame the business rationale for the audience who makes resource decisions. This is how you move from "we have issues" to "here is why we should invest."

Learning outcomes: 3E.01-04, 3F.01-03 | Source lessons: L20, L21

Component 5: Product Strategy Recommendation

Week 12

Draft a concrete recommendation for your product: acceptance criteria, roadmap input, or an early-intervention proposal. This is not "we should be more accessible." It is a specific, actionable recommendation connected to your product's development process.

Learning outcomes: 3G.01, 3G.02, 3G.03 | Source lesson: L22

Component 6: Stakeholder Connections

Weeks 11-14 (ongoing)

Introduce yourself to your Legal and Program Management counterparts. Document who they are, what their role covers, and one thing you learned about how accessibility decisions flow through your product's organization. This is a relationship, not an email.

Learning outcomes: 3E.01, 3F.01, 3G.01 | Source lessons: L20, L21, L22

PEAS Session Requirement

You must participate in at least one PEAS session during Weeks 11-14. Your PEAS Session Reflection (Component 2) is based on this experience.

Three participation modes are acceptable:

Mode 1: Organic

A PEAS session for your own product comes up. You participate as a reviewer or observer.

Mode 2: Observed

You join a PEAS session for another product as an observer.

Mode 3: Recorded

You watch a recorded PEAS session from the repository.

All three modes produce the same reflection artifact and are evaluated against the same rubric. Session assignments will be communicated by the instructor.

Final Portfolio Format

The six components are the evidence. How you present them is your choice. Your portfolio should tie the components together into a coherent story: here is the problem my product faces, here is the evidence, here is the case for action, here is what I contributed, and here is what I recommend.

Acceptable formats include:

  • A slide deck with narrative
  • A written report or document
  • A narrative website (AI-assisted tools are acceptable, and strongly recommended given Adobe's push for AI learning)
  • A video walkthrough
  • A combined format

The container is flexible. The evidence is not. Every format must include all six components with enough depth to evaluate against the rubric. Choose the format that lets you tell your product's story most effectively.

This flexibility follows the Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principle of Multiple Means of Action and Expression: the knowledge and skill you demonstrate is the same regardless of format.

Evaluation Rubric

Each component is scored on a 1-4 scale. You need a 2 or higher on every component to pass.

Score Level What It Means
4 Exemplary Exceeds expectations. Could be used as a model for future cohorts. Evidence is thorough, specific, and clearly connected to your product.
3 Proficient Meets all expectations. Evidence is complete, specific, and grounded in real data or practice.
2 Developing Meets minimum expectations. Evidence is present but may be generic or loosely connected to your product context.
1 Incomplete Does not meet expectations. Evidence is missing, generic, or disconnected from the learning outcomes. Requires revision.

Example: For Component 1 (Problem Definition), a 2 might identify three issues but classify them by WCAG criterion instead of by user impact. A 3 classifies by severity using the Sev 1-4 model and connects each finding to a blocked task. A 4 does all of that and identifies patterns across findings that point to a systemic root cause.

Total Score (out of 24) Result
20-24 Pass with Distinction
16-19 Pass with Merit
12-15 Pass
Any component at 1 Revise and Resubmit

Revise and Resubmit: If any component scores a 1, you will receive specific feedback on what is missing. You have one resubmission opportunity for the flagged components only. The resubmission deadline is one week after feedback is provided.
