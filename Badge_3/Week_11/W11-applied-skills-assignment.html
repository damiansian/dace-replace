Week 11 Applied Skills: Define the Problem + Contribute

Identify the accessibility problem your product faces and create something your team can use.

This week you begin building your capstone portfolio. The two components you produce here are the foundation everything else builds on: a clear picture of the problem and a tangible contribution to your team.

This week's lessons — L17 (Independent Review Methodology), L18 (Feedback and Critique), and L19 (Mentoring and Culture) — give you the methods. This assignment is where you apply them to your own product.

📊 Component 1: Problem Definition

Source lessons: L17 (Independent Review Methodology) | Outcomes: 3A.01, 3A.02, 3A.03

Using Jira data, the accessibility dashboard, or both, identify and document the accessibility problem your product faces. This is a data-driven analysis, not a list of opinions.

What to include:

  • Data source: State where your data comes from (Jira, dashboard, audit reports, or a combination) and the time range covered.
  • Findings: Identify the accessibility issues affecting your product. Group them in a way that reveals patterns — by component, by user impact, by severity, or by workflow.
  • Severity classification: Use the Sev 1-4 model to classify each finding. Connect severity to the user task that is blocked or degraded.
  • Summary statement: In 2-3 sentences, describe the overall accessibility posture of your product. What is the shape of the problem?

Rubric guidance:

A 2 identifies issues but classifies them generically (e.g., by WCAG criterion alone). A 3 classifies by severity using Sev 1-4 and connects findings to blocked user tasks. A 4 does all of that and identifies patterns across findings that point to a systemic root cause.

🛠️ Component 3: Contribution Artifact

Source lesson: L19 (Mentoring and Culture) | Outcomes: 3H.01, 3H.02, 3H.03

Create one reusable accessibility resource for your team. This is not a theoretical exercise — it should be something your team can pick up and use during real work.

Acceptable formats:

  • An accessibility checklist tailored to your team's component patterns
  • A pattern guide showing accessible implementations of components your team builds
  • Onboarding material for new designers joining your team
  • A shared example library with annotated accessible vs. inaccessible versions
  • A process improvement (e.g., a review checklist added to your team's design review workflow)

What makes it count:

  • Specific to your team: Generic checklists pulled from the internet do not qualify. It must address your team's actual patterns, tools, and workflows.
  • Usable: Another designer on your team could use this without asking you to explain it.
  • Contextualized: Include a brief note (2-3 sentences) explaining what gap this fills on your team and how you expect it to be used.

Rubric guidance:

A 2 produces a resource that is correct but generic. A 3 produces something clearly tailored to the team's context and ready to use. A 4 produces a resource that fills a documented gap, is already in use or shared with the team, and includes evidence of that adoption.
