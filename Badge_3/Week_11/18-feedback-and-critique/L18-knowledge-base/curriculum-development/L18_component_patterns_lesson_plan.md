# L18: Feedback and Critique - Lesson Plan

**Course:** DACE
**Badge:** Deliver
**Delivery:** In-person (instructor-led)
**Duration:** ~20 minutes (Week 11 session: 30 min review + 60 min across L17, L18, L19)
**Outcomes:** 3B.01, 3B.02, 3B.03

---

## Lesson Overview

This lesson develops the communication skills required for accessibility leadership. Learners already know how to identify issues. Now they need to deliver feedback that helps other designers understand the problem, stay engaged, and act effectively.

**Why This Matters:** Review quality is not just about technical accuracy. If the feedback is vague, harsh, or mismatched to the audience, the work stalls. Strong critique builds capability and trust. Weak critique creates avoidance and defensiveness.

**Connection to L17:** L17 taught learners how to conduct a review. L18 teaches them how to communicate the results of that review in ways that move the work forward.

## Content Outline

### Opening: Why Feedback Quality Matters (2 min)

**Key Teaching Points:**
- A technically correct comment can still fail if it is vague, accusatory, or poorly timed.
- The goal of critique is not to prove expertise. The goal is to make the work better.
- Accessibility leadership requires both rigor and interpersonal discipline.

**Transition:** "So what makes feedback actually useful?"

---

### Section 1: Constructive Feedback Frameworks (7 min)

**Outcomes:** 3B.01

**Key Teaching Points:**

1. **What constructive feedback sounds like**
   - Specific: identify the element, state, or flow in question
   - Actionable: make the next step clear
   - User-focused: explain the user impact, not just the rule
   - Kind: keep the conversation about the work, not the person

2. **SBI framework**
   - Situation: where the issue appears
   - Behavior: what the interface is doing
   - Impact: what happens to the user

3. **COIN framework**
   - Context: what the team is trying to accomplish
   - Observation: what you noticed
   - Impact: why it matters
   - Next: what should happen next

4. **Questioning vs telling**
   - Questions are useful when they help the designer reason
   - Direct answers are useful when the risk is clear or time is limited
   - Use the approach that increases understanding without obscuring the issue

**Micro-example:**
"On the settings modal, the Close button is only visible on hover. Keyboard users never discover it, which traps them in the dialog. We should make the control persistently visible and keep it in the tab order."

---

### Section 2: Facilitating Critique Sessions (7 min)

**Outcomes:** 3B.02

**Key Teaching Points:**

1. **Critique session structure**
   - Start with scope and goals
   - Move through findings in priority order
   - Keep the conversation anchored in user impact
   - End with clear next steps

2. **Psychological safety**
   - Make it possible for people to ask clarifying questions
   - Separate critique of the work from critique of the person
   - Use precise language instead of loaded language

3. **De-escalation patterns**
   - Re-anchor on user needs
   - Clarify that the issue is with the design behavior, not the designer
   - Narrow the conversation to the specific decision in question

4. **Participation management**
   - Avoid letting sessions drift into unrelated design debates
   - Keep higher-priority blockers visible
   - Make space for questions without losing the structure of the review

**Discussion prompt:**
"What makes accessibility critique feel punitive instead of useful?"

---

### Section 3: Adapting Communication for Skill Level (3 min)

**Outcomes:** 3B.03

**Key Teaching Points:**

1. **Audience calibration**
   - Discover Badge designer: explain the accessibility principle and the user impact
   - Define Badge designer: connect the issue to AT behavior and validation
   - Untrained designer: keep language concrete, avoid jargon, clarify the design consequence

2. **Mentoring moment vs review moment**
   - Mentoring moment: you are building understanding
   - Review moment: you are evaluating the work and defining action
   - Both matter, but they are not interchangeable

3. **Receiving feedback**
   - Badge 3 learners are also expected to take critique on their own reviews professionally
   - Modeling this behavior is part of accessibility leadership

---

### Close (1 min)

- "Strong accessibility critique is specific, actionable, and calibrated to the audience."
- "The best feedback increases both design quality and team capability."
- Transition to L19: "Next, we expand from critique in the room to building accessibility capability across the team."

L18 is reinforced through the ongoing Badge 3 homework tracks:

## Instructor Notes

### Pacing

This is a fast lesson. Keep it practical. The priority is that learners leave with reusable feedback patterns and a clear distinction between critique quality and technical correctness.

If running short:
- Reduce the discussion prompt
- Keep only one framework example instead of two

If running long:
- Use both SBI and COIN but keep the examples brief
- Add one quick role-play example of a defensive response and reframe

### Common Questions

**Q: Should I always use questions instead of direct feedback?**  
A: No. Use questions when they help the designer reason through the issue. Use direct guidance when the risk is clear and the next step needs to be explicit.

**Q: What if someone gets defensive?**  
A: Re-anchor on user impact, narrow the issue, and keep the conversation about the design behavior rather than the designer.

**Q: How detailed should critique be in a live session?**  
A: Give enough detail to clarify the issue and next step. Deep technical instruction can happen after the priority and action are understood.
