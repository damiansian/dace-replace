# L18 Quiz Questions: Feedback and Critique

**Competency:** 3B

## Question 1

**Outcome:** 3B.01
**Type:** Recall

**Stem:**
> Which combination best describes constructive accessibility feedback?

- A. Specific, actionable, user-focused, and kind
- B. Detailed, technical, exhaustive, and formal
- C. Direct, fast, personal, and opinionated
- D. Strict, standards-based, comprehensive, and final

**Correct Answer:** A
**Rationale:** Constructive accessibility feedback is specific enough to be useful, actionable enough to move the work forward, focused on user impact, and delivered in a way that preserves collaboration.

## Question 2

**Outcome:** 3B.01
**Type:** Application

**Stem:**
> A designer presents a sign-up form where the error text appears only after submission and is not associated with the relevant field. Which feedback example best uses an SBI-style structure?

- A. "This is not accessible. Please fix the errors."
- B. "When the user submits the sign-up form with missing required fields, the error text appears at the top of the page but is not tied to the field. That makes it harder for screen reader and magnification users to identify what needs to change."
- C. "Maybe review your form design because this could be better."
- D. "WCAG requires error handling, so this should probably be redesigned."

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** SBI structures feedback around the situation, the observed behavior, and the user impact. Option B identifies all three clearly and avoids vague or accusatory language.

## Question 3

**Outcome:** 3B.02
**Type:** Scenario

**Stem:**
> During an accessibility critique, a designer becomes defensive and says, "But the feature already works fine for most users." What is the best immediate facilitation move?

- A. Respond that compliance is mandatory and move on quickly
- B. Re-anchor the discussion on the affected users and the specific barrier being reviewed
- C. Ask the rest of the group whether they agree with the designer
- D. Stop the critique and defer all accessibility issues to asynchronous review

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** The most effective de-escalation move is to bring the discussion back to user impact and the specific issue. That keeps the conversation grounded in the work and reduces the chance that critique becomes personal.

---

## Question 4

**Outcome:** 3B.03
**Type:** Application

**Stem:**
> You are explaining a focus-order problem to a designer who has never taken DACE training. Which approach is most appropriate?

- A. Use AT-specific shorthand and assume they know the difference between browse order and focus order
- B. Keep the explanation concrete, describe what keyboard users experience, and avoid unnecessary jargon
- C. Skip the user explanation and just cite the WCAG number
- D. Give the same highly technical explanation you would give a Define Badge designer

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Communication should be calibrated to the audience. For an untrained designer, concrete language and clear user impact are more effective than dense technical terminology or isolated standards references.

---

## Question 5

**Outcome:** 3B.02
**Type:** Scenario

**Stem:**
> A critique session is running short. Several participants want to discuss visual polish details, but the primary checkout flow still has a keyboard blocker. What should the facilitator do?

- A. Let the discussion continue naturally so everyone feels heard
- B. Redirect the session back to the blocker and keep the group focused on priority issues
- C. End the session immediately without documenting any next steps
- D. Switch to discussing lower-priority items to reduce tension

**Correct Answer:** B
**Rationale:** Facilitation includes participation management and prioritization. When time is limited, the facilitator should keep the session anchored on the highest-impact accessibility issue and preserve clarity about next steps.
