Lesson 17: Independent Review Methodology

🚀 Deliver Badge 🔍 Module 3A

In Badges 1 and 2, you learned to find accessibility issues using checklists, Blueline annotations, and assistive technology testing. Now you learn to conduct independent reviews: assessing the full accessibility quality of a product feature, documenting your findings so they drive action, and prioritizing issues by real-world impact.

This is the shift from tester to reviewer. A tester checks items against a list. A reviewer assesses the experience holistically, finds patterns, and communicates findings that help teams make decisions.

💡 Key Concepts

From Tester to Reviewer 3A.01

In Badges 1 and 2, you learned to check individual items: Does this image have alt text? Does this button have an accessible name? Is the color contrast sufficient? Those skills are essential, but they are testing skills.

Reviewing is different. A reviewer assesses the overall accessibility quality of a feature or flow. You look at the full user journey, not isolated elements. You identify patterns rather than listing every instance of the same issue. You consider context: who uses this feature, what are they trying to accomplish, and what barriers stand in their way?

The mindset shift:

Tester (Badges 1-2) Reviewer (Badge 3)
Checks items against a checklist Assesses the full experience
Documents individual issues Identifies patterns of issues
Pass/fail per item Holistic quality assessment
Reports what is wrong Recommends a path forward
Checks isolated screens Evaluates the user journey

Key insight: You still use every skill from Badges 1 and 2. But now those skills are tools in your methodology, not the methodology itself.

Systematic Review Methodology 3A.01

Before you start a review, you need to scope it. Three questions:

1. What are you reviewing?

A full feature? A specific user flow? A single component? Define the scope before you start.

2. What fidelity level?

Early concept (big picture only), detailed design (full review), or pre-implementation (final check)?

3. How much time?

Time-boxing determines depth. A 30-minute review covers the critical path. A 2-hour review covers edge cases.

Review categories:

A systematic review covers these areas, drawing on everything you learned in Badges 1 and 2:

  • Visual: Color contrast, text sizing, spacing, reflow (L03, L06)
  • Structural: Headings, landmarks, reading order (L02, L07)
  • Interactive: Keyboard access, focus management, error handling (L05, L04)
  • Assistive technology: Accessible names, roles, states, announcements (L01, L09-L11)
  • Adaptive: Magnification, voice control, high contrast, user preferences, cognitive load (L12-L16)

The test for completeness:

Could a person using only a keyboard complete the task? Could a screen reader user understand the flow? Could a magnification user find all the controls? If you cannot answer yes to all three, the review is not complete.

  • I can scope a review by defining what to review, fidelity level, and time box
  • I can name the five review categories and which prior lessons each draws from
  • I understand how time-boxing affects review depth and scope decisions

PEAS Documentation 3A.02

PEAS (Product Equity Advisory Sessions) is Adobe's accessibility review process. The PE (Product Equity) team and inAccessible vendor (screen reader users) review designs together in a synchronous session.

The PEAS workflow:

  1. Request: Product team submits a PEAS Request Form (product name, business unit, design links, timeline)
  2. Session: PE team + inAccessible vendor review the design together, synchronously. Sessions are recorded with transcripts.
  3. Recap: Host completes a PEAS Host Recap Form with Session ID (PE- format), transcript link, outcome tier, and attendee lists

The four outcome tiers:

PEAS outcome tiers and their meanings for design teams
Tier Meaning
Complete No significant issues. Design can proceed.
One follow-up Minor issues. Schedule one follow-up session.
Async review Issues need async follow-up, not another live session.
Project Significant accessibility work needed. Becomes a project.

What makes review documentation good:

  • Specific: Point to the exact element and the exact problem
  • Contextual: Explain why it matters for users
  • Actionable: Suggest a path forward, not just "this is wrong"
  • Prioritized: Help the team know what to fix first

Remember: The recap form is lightweight. The real value is in how you communicate during the session and the clarity of your findings. Good documentation enables action. Bad documentation creates confusion.

✓ Checkpoint: PEAS documentation

  • I can describe the three steps of the PEAS workflow (request, session, recap)
  • I can name the four outcome tiers and explain what each means for the design team
  • I can explain what makes review documentation specific, contextual, actionable, and prioritized

Prioritizing by Impact and Risk 3A.03

Not all issues are equal. A missing accessible name on the primary action button is more urgent than a suboptimal heading level on a secondary page. Your job is to help the team fix the right things first.

Prioritization framework:

Priority Definition Example
Critical blocker User cannot complete the task at all Keyboard trap, no accessible name on primary action
High impact User can complete the task but with significant difficulty Missing focus management in a modal, unclear error messages
Improvement Functional but suboptimal experience Verbose screen reader announcements, missing descriptions
Best practice Not a failure but would improve the experience AAA criteria, enhanced ARIA patterns

Common mistake:

Equating WCAG level with priority. A Level A failure on a rarely-used settings page may be lower priority than a Level AA issue on the primary workflow. Priority = user impact x frequency of encounter x legal risk.

The reviewer's job:

Help the team make resource decisions. An unsorted list of 40 issues is not helpful. A prioritized list that says "fix these 3 blockers first, then address these 8 high-impact issues" gives the team a clear path forward.

✓ Checkpoint: Prioritization

  • I can classify findings using the four-level priority framework
  • I understand that WCAG level does not equal priority
  • I can explain how to weigh user impact, frequency, and legal risk
  • I can communicate priority rationale that helps teams make resource decisions

Putting It Together ALL

Here is your review workflow, from start to finish:

  1. Scope: Define what you are reviewing, the fidelity level, and how much time you have
  2. Review systematically: Work through the five categories (visual, structural, interactive, AT, adaptive) rather than ad-hoc checking
  3. Document findings: Be specific, contextual, actionable, and prioritized
  4. Identify patterns: Group related issues rather than listing every instance
  5. Prioritize: Classify by impact (critical blocker, high impact, improvement, best practice)
  6. Communicate: Present findings in a way that enables action, not defensiveness

Key takeaway:

A good review makes the path forward clear. Bad reviews just create defensive reactions.

  • I understand how to group findings into patterns
