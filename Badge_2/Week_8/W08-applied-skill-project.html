For both questions:

  • If possible, use real examples from your own work or at Adobe rather than manufacturing an example scenario.
  • Avoid strict definitions as questions; this is not about rote memorization, it is about challenging someone to think critically about the topic.
  • Ideal questions require an exam candidate to investigate, research, test, or perform some level of analysis.

📐 Structure Components

Stimulus (Optional/Context)

The foundational prompt. It can be a text passage, image, graph, case study, or scenario that provides the necessary information for a test-taker to analyze and answer a question. It serves as the context for evaluating critical thinking, knowledge application, or comprehension. In our work, it is likely a Figma file or existing interface that can be accessed by all and is stable (i.e., will not vary at test time).

Stem (Question/Statement)

The initial question, statement, or problem-posing phrase in a test item that dictates what the examinee must answer or solve. It acts as the prompt to which the examinee responds, often followed by choices in multiple-choice questions. Effective stems are clear, concise, and contain no cue. It can be a direct question or an incomplete statement. Positively phrased is preferred; avoid "except" or negative phrasing.

Key (Correct Answer)

The best or correct answer among the options. The correct response should NOT be the longest or most involved response as a clue to correctness.

Distractors (Incorrect Options)

Plausible but wrong answers designed to appeal to candidates who have not mastered the material. They should be realistic, similar in length, and grammatically consistent with the key. Ideal distractors are common misconceptions or things people mistake for correctness.

Rationale

Provide a defensible, researched, tested and validated rationale on why the correct response is correct. You can go an extra mile and rationalize why the incorrect responses are incorrect, but it is not required.
